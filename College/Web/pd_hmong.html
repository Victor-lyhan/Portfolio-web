Hmong Community Health Access & Parkinson’s Early Screening in Wisconsin

A mixed-methods, community co-design pilot with churches and interpreters to close language-and-trust gaps in early neurology screening. We ran 25 interviews, 2 focus groups (n=12), and 40 bilingual Hmong/EN app walk-throughs. Localization + community navigators improved outcomes: completion 42%→68%, SUS 59→76, and privacy drop-offs 31%→12%. Next: scale navigators, on-demand interpreters, and monthly church workshops.

Year: 2025
Link: Full research report

What did I do?

I met with Hmong families where they are—at home, church, and community spaces—to listen and know how they do. I learned their biggest barriers: language and fear of expensive tests. I then brainstorm and tested methods to make them more comfortable in using health tech like the one I developed.

Why did I do this?

I started this project after volunteering at the Wisconsin Parkinson’s Association conference and noticing how few minority families—especially Hmong—were there. I wanted to understand why: how they experience Parkinson’s, what blocks them from care, and how they feel about new tools. So I decided to listen first, then work with community members to co-create simple, culturally friendly education and earlier checks that actually reach the people being missed.

Methods

I interviewed and talked to Hmong families either in person or virtually. To get to know them better, I went to their markets, visited their house, joined their family dinners, and even helped them ... farm.

Executive Summary

What I learned at a glance

  • Problem. Hmong families face language, interpreter, and privacy-friction that delay early neurology screening.
  • Approach. Mixed-methods pilot with churches/interpreters: 25 interviews, 2 focus groups (n=12), and 40 bilingual app walk-throughs.
  • What changed. Bilingual UI + community navigators improved completion, usability, and reduced privacy drop-offs.
  • Do next. Launch on-demand interpreters, train navigators, embed bilingual consent/results, and run monthly church workshops.

Metric Baseline Post-Interv. Δ
Task completion 42% 68% +26 pp
SUS (0–100) 59 76 +17
Privacy drop-off 31% 12% −19 pp

Task completion: % of participants who fully finished app-based screening without assistance.
SUS (System Usability Scale): Standardized usability rating (0–100) — higher indicates smoother experience.
Privacy drop-off: % of users who stopped mid-screening due to privacy concerns or data-sharing hesitation.

Final Remark

Studies like this show how important it is to culturally tune the design and technology to raise completion and usability while reducing privacy drop-offs in terms of health-tech.
